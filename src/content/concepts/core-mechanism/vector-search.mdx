Once [embeddings](/concepts/embeddings) have turned text into vectors, retrieval is one
concrete operation: given a query vector, return the *k* nearest stored vectors by cosine
similarity. Doing it exactly is trivial — compare the query to every vector, sort, take the
top *k* — and it is exactly what a flat scan does. **Vector search** is the study of doing
that same thing *fast* when "every vector" means millions or billions of them: an
**approximate nearest-neighbor (ANN) index**, wrapped with storage, filtering, and CRUD,
is what a **vector database** is.

> **Mental model: a library catalogue.** Exact search is walking every shelf and reading
> every spine — you will always find the book, but the time grows with the collection. An
> ANN index is a catalogue that sends you straight to the few shelves most likely to hold
> it: dramatically faster, because you never visit the rest.
> **Where the analogy breaks:** the catalogue is *approximate* — if the book you wanted was
> shelved just across an aisle you were never sent down, you simply won't find it, and
> nothing tells you it was there. You can check more shelves (raise the "probe" or "ef"
> setting) to find more, at more cost; the dial between speed and completeness is the whole
> game, and a badly-tuned index drops relevant results silently.

## What problem existed before this?

With embeddings in hand, the naive retriever is a loop: score the query against every
stored vector and keep the best few. This **flat** (brute-force) search is exact — it
always returns the true nearest neighbors — and its cost is one comparison per stored
vector, per query. That is completely fine for thousands or even millions of vectors. It
stops being fine when the corpus is very large *and* queries are frequent: scanning a
hundred million vectors for every one of thousands of queries per second is too slow and
too expensive to do exhaustively.

## Why was this invented?

The insight: you rarely need the *provably* nearest neighbor — you need *good* neighbors,
fast. **Approximate nearest-neighbor (ANN)** indexes trade a little accuracy for a large
speedup by structuring the vectors so a query only has to look at a small, promising slice
of the corpus. Two families dominate. **IVF** ("inverted file") clusters the vectors and,
at query time, only scans the few clusters nearest the query. **HNSW** builds a navigable
graph and walks it greedily toward the query's neighborhood. Both do the same trick: *look
at less of the corpus*. A **vector database** packages such an index with persistence,
metadata filtering ("nearest vectors *where* `tenant = X`"), updates, and scaling.

## How does it work?

Flat search compares the query to every vector. An IVF-style index instead compares the
query to a handful of cluster **centroids**, opens only the nearest cluster(s), and ranks
just their members — far fewer comparisons. The catch is visible below: a true neighbor
sitting in a cluster you didn't open is missed, so **recall** (the fraction of the true
top-*k* you actually return) drops below 1. Opening more clusters recovers it, at more
cost. Every number here — comparison counts, cosine scores, recall — is computed by the
real [`src/lib/retrieval`](https://github.com/ZeroCool989/agent-atlas/blob/main/src/lib/retrieval/vector-index.ts)
index over a tiny illustrative space (the *vectors* are hand-placed so the trade-off is
visible; the *counting* is exact):

At this toy scale the approximate index isn't even worth it — probing every cluster costs
*more* than the flat scan, because the overhead (comparing centroids) outweighs any saving.
That is honest and important: ANN wins only when the corpus is large enough that skipping
most of it saves far more than the index costs. HNSW works differently in the details
(graph hops, not clusters) but exposes the identical dial — its `ef` parameter is IVF's
`nProbe`: search wider for higher recall, narrower for lower latency.

## Is it actually necessary?

Nearest-neighbor search is necessary the moment you retrieve with embeddings — but an
**approximate index or a dedicated vector database usually is not.** For thousands to low
millions of vectors, a flat cosine scan over an in-memory array is exact, trivially
correct, and fast enough; you do not need a new service. The index earns its keep at scale:
large corpora, high query rates, tight latency budgets, or a need for persistence, metadata
filtering, and live updates that an array doesn't give you.

## What are the trade-offs?

The core trade-off is **recall versus latency**, and it is a dial, not a fixed point. A
lower probe/`ef` setting is faster and cheaper but returns fewer of the true neighbors; a
higher setting recovers recall at more cost. Around that sit the usual index trade-offs:
memory (graph indexes like HNSW are memory-hungry), index **build time**, and **staleness**
— inserts, deletes, and updates must reach the index, or retrieval silently returns results
that no longer reflect the data. And because the results are approximate, they can be
slightly non-deterministic across index versions or builds.

## What complexity does it introduce?

- **An index to build and tune.** Choosing IVF vs HNSW, cluster counts or graph parameters,
  and the recall/latency operating point is real work, and the right setting is
  corpus-specific — measured with a recall benchmark, not guessed.
- **A service to operate** (if you use a vector database rather than an embedded library):
  another system to deploy, scale, back up, secure, and pay for.
- **Metadata filtering interactions.** "Nearest vectors where `field = X`" is subtler than
  it looks — pre- vs post-filtering changes both recall and latency, and a naive filter can
  quietly gut recall.
- **Freshness and deletes.** Keeping the index in sync with the source data — including
  *removing* vectors when their source is deleted — is an ongoing obligation, not a one-time
  load.

## What simpler alternative exists?

**A flat brute-force scan** — exactly the `FlatIndex` in this repo: cosine against every
vector, sort, take *k*. Exact, no service, no tuning, and correct by construction; the right
default until you have measured that it is too slow. Between "an array" and "a dedicated
vector database" sit two more modest steps worth trying first: an **embedded ANN library**
(FAISS, hnswlib) inside your own process — the index without the service — and
**`pgvector`** or a similar extension that adds vector search to a database you already run.
Reach for a standalone vector database when scale, query rate, or operational needs
genuinely outgrow those.

## When should I use it?

When the corpus is large enough that a flat scan misses your latency or cost budget; when
query volume is high; when you need vector search *plus* metadata filtering, persistence,
and live inserts/deletes/updates together; and when you can define an acceptable recall
target and tune to it. In short: at the scale where "look at everything, every query" stops
being affordable.

## When should I avoid it?

When the corpus is small (a flat scan is simpler *and* exact). When exact nearest neighbors
are mandatory and a missed result is unacceptable — approximate indexes can silently drop
one. When a library or `pgvector` would do and a separate service is pure operational
overhead. And whenever you haven't yet *measured* that flat search is too slow — adding an
approximate index you didn't need is complexity, tuning burden, and a new failure mode
(silent recall loss) bought for nothing.

## The engine, in a few readable functions

The whole thing is small and dependency-free:
[`vector-index.ts`](https://github.com/ZeroCool989/agent-atlas/blob/main/src/lib/retrieval/vector-index.ts)
builds on the cosine core from embeddings and adds a `FlatIndex` (exact, scans everything)
and a `ClusteredIndex` (IVF-style: bucket by centroid, probe the nearest clusters), plus a
`recall` helper. Read `ClusteredIndex.search` and watch the comparison count fall — and the
recall with it, until you raise `nProbe`. That is the exact trade-off every vector database
sells you a dial for.

## Governance

A vector index is infrastructure that holds derived personal data, so it inherits real
obligations:

- **Deletion has to reach the index.** Under **GDPR**, honoring a deletion request means the
  person's vectors must leave the vector store too, not just the source table — an index
  that quietly retains deleted content is a compliance gap, and approximate indexes can make
  deletes harder to guarantee.
- **Data residency and access.** Where the vector database runs, and who can query it, is
  part of the data-governance story — a hosted vector-DB service is a processor holding
  data derived from your users.
- **Approximate ≠ reproducible.** When retrieval feeds a consequential decision, "the index
  returned a slightly different set this build" matters for auditability; you may need to
  pin index versions or fall back to exact search for the record.
- **Applicability is conditional.** Whether GDPR, the EU AI Act, or sector rules bite
  depends on the data, use case, role, and jurisdiction — a vector index doesn't trigger a
  framework by itself. What it creates is a *surface* you must account for: what vectors it
  holds, where, and how completely you can change or remove them.

A language model answers from its weights — a compression of its training data, frozen at
some cutoff. That memory is confident but unciteable, can't include your private documents,
and goes stale the day training stops. **Retrieval-augmented generation** changes where the
answer comes from: instead of asking the model to recall, you first *retrieve* relevant text
from a corpus you control, put it in the prompt, and ask the model to answer from that
evidence and cite it. RAG isn't a new algorithm — it's a composition of things you already
have: embeddings and vector search to find the text, context assembly to place it, the model
to phrase the answer.

> **Mental model: an open-book exam.** Closed-book, the model answers from memory — fast, but
> it can't show its work and it bluffs when unsure. RAG hands it the book, open to the right
> page, and says "answer from this, and cite the page." **Where the analogy breaks:** the
> model can still misread the page, cite the wrong one, or confidently answer from memory
> anyway; and if the book doesn't contain the answer, an honest student says "it's not in
> here" — which a RAG system only does if you *build* that refusal. The open book raises the
> ceiling on honesty; it doesn't guarantee it.

## What problem existed before this?

To make a model "know" something it didn't learn, your options were bad. **Retrain or
fine-tune** it on the new knowledge: slow, expensive, and fine-tuning teaches *style and
format* far more reliably than it injects *facts* — and the moment the facts change you do it
again. Or **rely on parametric memory** and accept the failure modes: no knowledge past the
training cutoff, nothing about your private or proprietary data, no way to cite a source, and
hallucination stated as fluently as truth. A support bot that "knows" your product only from
generic pretraining is guessing, and you can't tell when.

## Why was this invented?

The insight ([Lewis et al., 2020](https://arxiv.org/abs/2005.11401)) was to **separate
knowledge from the model**. Keep the model for what it's good at — language and reasoning —
and move the facts into an external corpus you can update, audit, and search. At query time,
retrieve the passages relevant to the question and condition generation on them. Knowledge
becomes data you own and edit, not weights you retrain; adding a document is an insert, not a
training run; and because the answer is built from specific retrieved passages, it can carry
citations back to them.

## How does it work?

A pipeline, run once to *index* and again per *query*:

1. **Chunk** the documents into passages small enough to retrieve precisely.
2. **Embed** each chunk into a vector (the [Embeddings](/concepts/embeddings) concept).
3. **Index** the vectors for fast nearest-neighbor search ([Vector search](/concepts/vector-search)).
4. At query time, **embed the question** and **retrieve** the top-k nearest chunks.
5. **Assemble** those chunks into the prompt with an instruction to answer only from them and
   cite — they now spend part of the [context window](/concepts/context-windows).
6. **Generate** the answer, grounded and cited.

## Is it actually necessary?

When the answer depends on knowledge the model can't have in its weights — your private docs,
anything after its cutoff, facts that change — and you need it *citable*, RAG is the honest
tool. When the knowledge is small and static enough to sit in the prompt directly, or the
model already knows it, or a clearer question would do, RAG is a pipeline you're building for
no reason. It earns its complexity when knowledge is large, changing, private, or must be
sourced — not before.

## What are the trade-offs?

RAG buys **fresh, private, updatable, citable** knowledge without retraining. It charges:

- **Retrieval is the ceiling.** Answer quality is bounded by what you retrieved; the model
  can't use evidence it never saw. Garbage retrieval, garbage answer — fluently phrased.
- **Distractors.** Retrieve a plausible-but-irrelevant passage and the model may anchor on
  it. More context is not always better context.
- **Grounding is not a guarantee.** The model can still ignore the passages and answer from
  memory, misread them, or cite the wrong one. Citations make errors *checkable*, not absent.
- **It competes for the window.** Retrieved context spends tokens that could hold the
  conversation or the output (see the budget bar above).

## What complexity does it introduce?

Every stage is a real system with its own failure surface: a **chunker** (size and boundaries
quietly decide what can be retrieved at all), an **embedding model** to run and version, a
**vector index** to store and tune, a **retriever** whose k and thresholds you must set, the
**context assembly**, and — the part teams skip — an **eval set** to know if any of it works.
You are now maintaining a search system, and search is hard. The most common RAG failure isn't
the model; it's retrieval that quietly returns the wrong chunks and no one measures it.

## What simpler alternative exists?

Climb the ladder only as far as you must:

- **The model already knows.** For common knowledge, just ask — no retrieval.
- **Long-context stuffing.** If the relevant documents fit in the window, put them straight in
  the prompt. No index, no retriever, no chunking — and no retrieval errors. Windows have grown
  enough that this is often the right first move for a handful of documents.
- **A better prompt.** Sometimes "answer only from this text" over pasted content is the whole
  job.
- **Fine-tuning** for *behaviour and format*, not facts.

RAG is what you reach for when the corpus is too big for the window, changes too often to bake
in, or must be sourced. And note the distinction: **RAG is a fixed retrieve-then-generate
workflow**; letting the model *decide* when to search is a [tool-using agent](/concepts/tool-calling)
— same retrieval underneath, more autonomy (and less predictability) on top.

## When should I use it?

Question-answering over documentation, policies, tickets, or a knowledge base; support and
internal-search assistants; anything that must answer from current or private information and
show its sources. Especially where being able to point at *why* the system said something is a
requirement, not a nicety.

## When should I avoid it?

When the knowledge is small and stable enough to live in the prompt or the weights; when you
can't keep the corpus fresh and correct (a stale index is confidently wrong); when the task
needs exact, deterministic answers rather than retrieved-and-phrased ones; when the latency of
a retrieval hop breaks the experience; or when you haven't yet *measured* that the model
answering directly is insufficient. Don't build a search system to answer questions a plain
prompt already handles.

## The pipeline, in a few readable functions

The whole thing is a composition, not a black box:
[`src/lib/rag/pipeline.ts`](https://github.com/ZeroCool989/agent-atlas/blob/main/src/lib/rag/pipeline.ts)
— `retrieve` (reuses `cosineSimilarity` from the retrieval engine), `assembleRagPrompt`
(reuses the prompt assembler and its token budget), and `groundedAnswer`, which does the one
thing many production systems forget: **it refuses when the best passage is below a relevance
floor** rather than grounding an answer on an irrelevant chunk. Read it and you'll see RAG has
no magic in it — it is embeddings + vector search + prompt assembly, wired in a line, plus the
discipline to say "not in the context."

## Governance

Retrieval turns a corpus into part of the system's answers, which pulls it into scope:

- **Retrieved personal data is still processing.** Putting someone's record into the prompt to
  answer a question is, under **GDPR**, processing personal data — lawful basis, access, and
  deletion obligations follow the corpus, and "we only retrieved it" is not an exemption.
- **Provenance is an asset here, not just a risk.** Because RAG answers cite their sources, it
  can *support* transparency and traceability expectations (e.g. EU AI Act documentation
  duties) better than a closed-book model — if the citations are real and checked.
- **The corpus is an attack surface.** Whatever you retrieve enters the prompt, so a poisoned
  or injected document is a path to prompt injection and data exfiltration; retrieval doesn't
  sanitise content, it delivers it.
- **Where the vectors and documents live** (residency, retention, access) is a data-governance
  question the moment the corpus contains regulated data. As always, whether a specific
  framework bites depends on the data, use, role, and jurisdiction — RAG doesn't trigger one by
  itself, but it defines the surface you must be able to account for.

A language model reasons over tokens, but tokens are just ids — `automobile` and `car`
are as unrelated to a string comparison as `automobile` and `Tuesday`. **Embeddings**
fix that by turning a piece of text into a vector of numbers — a point in a
high-dimensional space — arranged by a trained model so that texts with similar *meaning*
land in similar *directions*. Once meaning is a coordinate, "find the most similar text"
becomes arithmetic you can compute and rank: this is the mechanism under semantic search,
retrieval for RAG, clustering, deduplication, and recommendations.

> **Mental model: a map of meaning.** An embedding model is a cartographer that has read
> an enormous amount of text and placed every idea on a map, so that things used in
> similar ways sit near each other — royalty in one region, animals in another. To find
> what's related to a query, you drop it on the map and look at its neighbors.
> **Where the analogy breaks:** the map is *learned from data*, so it inherits that
> data's blind spots and biases; "near" means "similar in the training distribution's
> sense," which is not the same as "true," "current," or "relevant to your exact need";
> and the map is approximate — two things can be closer than they should be. The geometry
> is real and computable; what the coordinates *mean* is only as good as the model that
> drew them.

## What problem existed before this?

Before embeddings, finding text meant matching words. Lexical search (TF-IDF, BM25) is
excellent at this and still is — but it can only match the *terms you typed*. Ask for
"how do I cancel my subscription" and a document titled "ending your membership" is
invisible, because they share almost no words. Synonyms, paraphrases, translations, and
"same idea, different phrasing" are all outside what exact matching can see. You could
search only for text whose words you already knew.

## Why was this invented?

The idea is the **distributional hypothesis**: words that appear in similar contexts tend
to mean similar things. Train a model to predict words from their neighbors and it is
forced to give words used in similar ways similar internal representations — vectors. Word
embeddings ([word2vec](https://arxiv.org/abs/1301.3781), 2013) demonstrated that these
learned vectors captured real structure, and the idea generalized from words to sentences,
paragraphs, and whole documents. The payoff: meaning becomes a vector, and comparing
meaning becomes comparing vectors — cheap, rankable arithmetic instead of an impossible
string match.

## How does it work?

Three steps: **embed** the query and every candidate into vectors with the same model;
**score** each candidate by how aligned its vector is with the query's, using cosine
similarity (the cosine of the angle between them, `1` = same direction, `0` = unrelated,
`-1` = opposite); **rank** by that score and keep the top few. Watch it run — each score
below is computed by the real [`src/lib/retrieval`](https://github.com/ZeroCool989/agent-atlas/blob/main/src/lib/retrieval/embeddings.ts)
engine over a tiny illustrative space (the *vectors* are hand-placed so the geometry is
visible on a page; the *math* is exactly what a production retriever runs):

Real embeddings live in hundreds or thousands of dimensions, not two, and are produced by
a trained model rather than placed by hand — but the operation is identical: embed, score
by cosine, rank. That last step, "rank a corpus by similarity to a query," *is* nearest-
neighbor search, and it is the whole engine of retrieval.

## Is it actually necessary?

For matching by *meaning* over a large corpus — yes, there is no lexical trick that sees
paraphrase and synonymy. For everything else, often no. If your users search with the
exact terms that appear in your documents, if the corpus is small, or if you need exact
matches on names, codes, or identifiers, keyword search is simpler, faster, and frequently
*more* accurate. Embeddings earn their complexity when meaning, not wording, is what you
need to match.

## What are the trade-offs?

Embeddings buy semantic reach — synonyms, paraphrase, cross-lingual matches — and charge
approximation and opacity. A cosine score is a similarity, not a relevance judgment and
certainly not a truth judgment: the nearest neighbor can still be wrong, off-topic, or
outdated, and you cannot read *why* two vectors are close. You also inherit the embedding
model's biases, its training cutoff, and its notion of "similar," none of which you can
inspect. And retrieval quality is capped by upstream choices — the embedding model and how
you split text into chunks — as much as by the math.

## What complexity does it introduce?

Real moving parts, each with a cost:

- **An embedding model** — another dependency to run or call, with latency and (often)
  per-token cost, and a version you must pin (re-embedding everything when it changes).
- **A vector store / index** — somewhere to keep the vectors and search them quickly. At
  scale that means an approximate-nearest-neighbor index (HNSW, IVF), which trades exact
  ranking for speed and adds tuning.
- **Chunking** — documents must be split before embedding; chunk size and boundaries
  quietly determine what can be retrieved (this is where much RAG quality is won or lost).
- **Freshness** — embeddings drift out of sync with content; changed documents must be
  re-embedded, or retrieval silently returns stale meaning.

## What simpler alternative exists?

**Lexical search — BM25 or TF-IDF.** No model, no vector store, exact and interpretable,
and a genuinely strong baseline: for queries built from the documents' own terms it can
beat embeddings outright. In practice the strongest systems are often **hybrid** — run
lexical and vector search together and combine the results — precisely because each covers
the other's blind spot (lexical nails exact terms and rare tokens; vectors catch
paraphrase). Reach for embeddings when you have measured that lexical search misses
meaning your users need; not before.

## When should I use it?

Semantic search and RAG retrieval; clustering and deduplication ("are these two tickets the
same issue?"); recommendation and "more like this"; any time the match is about *meaning*
over a corpus too large to read, and wording varies. Almost always worth pairing with
lexical search rather than replacing it.

## When should I avoid it?

When exact matching is the actual requirement — identifiers, product codes, legal citations,
names — where a near-miss is a wrong answer. When the corpus is small enough to match or
even read directly. When keyword search already satisfies users (measure before adding a
model). When you cannot keep embeddings fresh, or cannot tolerate an unexplainable ranking
in a setting that demands interpretability.

## The engine, in a few readable functions

The whole retrieval core is small and dependency-free:
[`embeddings.ts`](https://github.com/ZeroCool989/agent-atlas/blob/main/src/lib/retrieval/embeddings.ts)
— `cosineSimilarity` (direction-only, scale-invariant, zero-guarded) and
`nearestNeighbors` (rank a corpus, deterministic tie-breaking). That `nearestNeighbors`
function is the literal operation a vector database performs, only approximately and over
far more vectors. Read it, then note what it does *not* do: it returns *similar*, never
*correct*. Everything vector search and RAG add is built on exactly this.

## Governance

Embeddings are a data-protection surface, not a neutral transform:

- **Derived personal data is still personal data.** Embedding someone's message, review,
  or record produces a vector *derived from* personal data — under **GDPR** that is still
  processing, still needs a lawful basis, and a store of user-derived vectors is in scope
  for access, deletion, and breach obligations. An embedding is not anonymization: it can
  retain and leak information about its source.
- **Provenance of the corpus.** What you embedded, and your right to use it, is part of the
  data-governance story for any retrieval system — and it differs for a curated knowledge
  base versus scraped or user-supplied text.
- **Bias becomes retrieval behavior.** A model's learned associations decide what gets
  retrieved; skew in the embedding space can surface as skewed or discriminatory results,
  which is a fairness concern wherever the retrieval feeds a consequential decision.
- **Applicability is conditional.** Whether GDPR, the EU AI Act, or sector rules bite
  depends on the data, the use case, your role, and the jurisdiction — embeddings don't
  trigger a framework by themselves. What they determine is the *surface* you must be able
  to account for: what you embedded, where the vectors live, and how retrieval behaves.

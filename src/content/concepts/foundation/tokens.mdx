A language model never sees your words. Before any "thinking" happens, your text is cut
into **tokens** — chunks from a fixed vocabulary, each mapped to a number — and those
numbers are all the model ever receives. Everything practical about working with LLMs
runs through this fact: what you pay, what fits in the context window, and a whole
family of strange failures.

> **Mental model: Lego bricks.** A tokenizer owns a fixed box of bricks — some tiny
> (single characters), some pre-assembled chunks (` token`, `ing`) — and it rebuilds
> any text you give it out of exactly those bricks. Common patterns get big
> pre-assembled bricks; rare words get snapped together from small pieces.
> **Where the analogy breaks:** Lego bricks are designed by someone; token "bricks" are
> *learned automatically from frequency statistics*, and no engineer chooses them. The
> box also differs per model — another vendor's set has different bricks entirely.

## What problem existed before this?

Neural networks compute over numbers, so every text model needs a **fixed, finite
vocabulary**: a numbered list of symbols it can read and emit. Both obvious vocabularies
fail:

- **Characters** — a tiny, complete vocabulary (nothing is ever out of vocabulary), but
  sequences become enormous and each symbol carries almost no meaning. The model spends
  its capacity learning to spell.
- **Whole words** — meaningful symbols and short sequences, but the vocabulary is
  unbounded (names, typos, new coinages, morphology, other languages). Every unseen word
  becomes the same unknown symbol: information destroyed at the front door.

Machine translation hit this wall hardest in the 2010s: rare and novel words were
exactly the ones that mattered, and word-level systems couldn't represent them at all.

## Why was this invented?

Subword tokenization is the working compromise: keep a *bounded* vocabulary, but let
its entries be **fragments of words learned from data**. Sennrich, Haddow & Birch
(2016) adapted a 1994 compression algorithm — Byte Pair Encoding — to do this: start
from characters, then repeatedly merge the most frequent adjacent pair into a new
vocabulary entry. Frequent words end up as single tokens; rare words remain decomposable
into pieces. Nothing is ever unrepresentable, and the vocabulary size is a dial you
choose.

## How does it work?

Two phases, both mechanical:

1. **Training** (once, offline): count all adjacent symbol pairs in a corpus → merge
   the most frequent pair into a new symbol → repeat, a few thousand times. The ordered
   list of merges *is* the tokenizer.
2. **Encoding** (every request): split text into characters, then apply the learned
   merges — always the earliest-learned applicable merge first — until none applies.
   Look up each resulting symbol's id.

## Is it actually necessary?

Understanding it: yes, unconditionally — it is the unit of billing, context limits, and
latency for every LLM you will ever call, and you cannot opt out. *Implementing* it:
almost never in production (every model ships its tokenizer), but building one once is
the fastest route to real understanding, which is why the tiny trainer above is this
platform's first build project.

## What are the trade-offs?

The vocabulary size is a genuine dial with real stakes. **Bigger vocabulary** → shorter
sequences (cheaper attention, more fits in the window) but a larger embedding matrix
and rarer tokens that train poorly. **Smaller vocabulary** → more robust pieces but
longer, costlier sequences. And because merges are *learned from a specific corpus*,
tokenization inherits that corpus's biases: English compresses well; morphologically
rich or underrepresented languages fragment into many more tokens — the same sentence
can cost several times more depending on language. Code, numbers, and technical jargon
fragment in their own characteristic ways.

## What complexity does it introduce?

An invisible preprocessing layer you must now reason about:

- **Counting requires the tokenizer.** You cannot reliably estimate tokens from words
  or characters — budgets and truncation logic need real counts from the model's own
  tokenizer.
- **Sub-token blindness.** The model receives ids, not letters — this is why models
  miscount the r's in "strawberry": the letters aren't there to count.
- **Boundary artifacts.** ` token` ≠ `token`; trailing spaces, casing, and punctuation
  shift boundaries and can shift behavior.
- **Cross-model incompatibility.** Counts, ids, and boundaries are per-tokenizer;
  numbers computed for one model are wrong for another (see misconceptions below).

## What simpler alternative exists?

Character-level models genuinely work — the trade is longer sequences for a simpler,
never-surprised vocabulary, and for some domains (DNA, some morphology-heavy tasks,
tiny models) that trade is right. Byte-level BPE (GPT-2's variant of exactly what you
watched above) is the production refinement: start from all 256 bytes instead of
characters, so *nothing* — no emoji, no script — is ever out of vocabulary. There is no
meaningful "simpler than subword" option for general LLMs; the alternatives sit on the
same dial.

## When should I use it?

Token-level thinking, always: budget context deliberately (see
[context windows](/concepts/context-windows)), measure costs in tokens per request,
and when counting matters, count with **the target model's own tokenizer** — not a
rule of thumb, not another model's.

## When should I avoid it?

Avoid transplanting token intuitions across models — a prompt "worth 800 tokens" for
one tokenizer may be 1,100 for another. Avoid character-count heuristics (≈4
chars/token) anywhere money or truncation depends on the number; they drift most
exactly where it hurts (code, non-English text). And avoid hand-rolling a tokenizer in
production: match the model, use its official one.

## Common misconceptions

- **"A token is a word."** Sometimes — frequent English words earn single tokens. But
  you watched `tokenization` stay in five pieces while ` token` was one. Token ≈ word
  is exactly as wrong as your text is unusual.
- **"A token is a character."** Only at the floor. Characters are the *starting point*
  BPE merges away from; most tokens span several.
- **"Every model uses the same tokenizer."** No — vocabulary, merges, and ids are
  training artifacts. The playground's ids above are meaningless to any other model.
- **"Token counts are predictable from word counts."** Only for text resembling the
  training corpus. The playground's "unseen word" experiment shows the same word count
  producing very different token counts.
- **"Tokenization treats languages equally."** It compresses what its corpus contained.
  Identical meaning can cost multiples more in tokens across languages — a fairness
  *and* a budgeting issue.

## Teaching model vs production vs research

Honesty about what you just watched:

| | This lesson's tokenizer | Production (GPT-2/4-class) | Research frontier |
|---|---|---|---|
| Base symbols | characters seen in corpus | all 256 bytes (nothing unknown) | bytes, or none |
| Vocabulary | 36 entries, 16 merges | 50k–200k entries | varies |
| Training data | 7 hand-written lines | terabytes of web text | — |
| Algorithm | **BPE — the real one** | BPE variants (byte-level, regex pre-splits) + optimized implementations | tokenizer-free / learned segmentation research |

The *algorithm* is not simplified — only its diet. Everything you observed (merge
order, leading spaces, fragmentation of rare words) happens identically at production
scale, just 10,000× bigger.

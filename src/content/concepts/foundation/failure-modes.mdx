A language model is a [next-token predictor](/concepts/what-is-a-language-model): it emits
text that is *plausible* given its training, with no separate signal for whether that text
is *true*. When plausible and true coincide, it looks brilliant. When they diverge, it
produces a confident, fluent, well-formed statement that is simply false — a
**hallucination**. This is not a defect bolted onto an otherwise-truthful system; it is the
direct consequence of what the model optimises for. Understanding that is the difference
between engineering around the problem and being surprised by it in production.

> **Mental model: a brilliant improviser with no fact-checker.** Picture someone who has
> read almost everything and can continue any sentence in a fitting, confident voice — but
> has no notion of "I'm not sure" and never says it. **Where the analogy breaks:** a human
> improviser knows when they're bluffing; the model has no internal "truth" variable at
> all, so its confident-sounding tone carries exactly zero information about correctness.

## What problem existed before this?

Earlier NLP systems failed *loudly*: a rule-based parser threw an error, a classifier
returned low confidence, a search engine returned nothing. You could tell when they didn't
know. Generative language models removed that signal. They always produce fluent output,
and the fluency is uniform whether the content is right or invented — so the failure became
*silent and disguised as success*. The new problem is not "the model breaks" but "the model
is smoothly, plausibly wrong and looks exactly like when it's right."

## Why was this invented?

Nobody invented hallucination on purpose — it falls directly out of the training objective.
A model rewarded for predicting the most probable next token learns to produce text that
*fits the patterns of its corpus*, not text that corresponds to reality. There is no
ground-truth-of-the-world term in the loss, only "does this look like the training data."
Alignment (instruction tuning, RLHF) makes models more helpful and better-behaved, but it
optimises for *human-preferred* responses — which can actively make things worse in one
specific way: **sycophancy**, the tendency to agree with the user or tell them what they
seem to want, because agreeable answers were rewarded. So the very training that makes the
output useful also bakes in its failure modes.

## How does it work?

The core failures share one root — plausibility is not truth — but show up in distinct,
recognisable shapes worth naming:

- **Factual hallucination:** a confident claim that is simply false.
- **Fabricated sources:** invented citations, URLs, quotes, or case law that *look*
  authoritative — a favourite because the format is easy to imitate.
- **Sycophancy:** agreeing with a wrong premise in the prompt, or reversing a correct
  answer when the user pushes back.
- **Instruction drift & brittleness:** obeying then quietly abandoning a constraint over a
  long output, or flipping behaviour on a trivial rewording of the prompt.
- **Fluent reasoning errors:** a wrong chain of steps that survives review *because* the
  prose is confident and well-structured.

You cannot detect these by reading, because reading is exactly the sense fluency fools. You
detect them with deterministic checks. Walk through the cheapest, most effective one — does
each cited source actually exist in what we retrieved?

The checker reliably catches the two easy failures: a **fabricated citation** (an id that
was never retrieved) and an **uncited claim**. The last step is the important one: a
sentence whose citation *resolves* is marked "supported" even though that source does not
actually back the claim. Catching a made-up citation is simple string work; verifying that
a real citation *supports* its statement is the hard, unsolved part — a smaller version of
the whole problem.

## Is it actually necessary?

Understanding this is unconditionally necessary — it is the risk model behind every LLM
feature. What's optional is *how much you spend defending against it*, and that scales with
stakes. A brainstorming toy can tolerate the occasional confident fib; a system that
answers medical, legal, or financial questions cannot, and needs grounding, verification,
and human review proportional to the harm a wrong answer causes. The mistake is treating a
high-stakes deployment with low-stakes caution because a demo looked convincing.

## What are the trade-offs?

- **Usefulness vs. trust.** The generative flexibility that makes the model useful is the
  same mechanism that lets it be fluently wrong; you cannot keep one and delete the other.
- **Mitigation cost vs. residual risk.** Grounding, verification, self-consistency, and
  human review each cut the error rate — at the cost of latency, money, and complexity —
  and each leaves a residual you must still plan for.
- **Refusal vs. helpfulness.** Tuning a model to say "I don't know" more often reduces
  confident errors but also produces unhelpful refusals on things it did know. There is no
  setting that is only upside.

## What complexity does it introduce?

- **You must design for wrong output as the normal case, not the exception** — every
  downstream step needs to assume the text may be false.
- **Detection is itself hard.** The reliable checks are the narrow, deterministic ones
  (does the citation resolve? does the JSON validate? does the number fall in range?).
  Checking *semantic* truth generally requires another fallible model or a human.
- **Evaluation must be built.** "It looked fine in the demo" is not measurement; catching
  regressions in a probabilistic system means an [eval](/concepts/tool-calling) set and
  scored runs, not spot-checks.
- **Mitigations interact.** Retrieval reduces fabrication but can introduce
  mis-attribution; lower temperature reduces wild errors but can entrench a confident wrong
  answer. There is no single knob.

## What simpler alternative exists?

If the task has a checkable structure, prefer a mechanism that *can* be verified over free
prose that can't. Extract structured data and [validate it](/concepts/structured-outputs)
rather than trusting a paragraph. Retrieve facts and cite them
([RAG](/concepts/rag)) rather than asking the model to recall them. For anything with a
deterministic answer, don't use a generative model at all. The simplest reliable pattern is
"narrow the surface": the less open-ended text you trust, the fewer places a hallucination
can hide.

## When should I use it?

This is a lens you apply, not a feature you switch on — so "use it" means: **assume it,
always.** Whenever a language model's output influences a decision, ask where a confident
wrong answer would go and what would catch it. Reach hardest for the mitigations —
grounding, output validation, citation checks, self-consistency, human-in-the-loop — in
proportion to how much a wrong answer costs.

## When should I avoid it?

Avoid two opposite mistakes. First, do **not** promise anyone you have "eliminated"
hallucination — you can reduce, bound, and detect it, and claiming more is the failure mode
that gets teams (and their compliance officers) into trouble. Second, do not over-engineer a
low-stakes feature into paralysis: a creative-writing helper does not need a citation
pipeline. Match the defence to the stakes — neither pretend the problem is solved, nor
treat every use as life-critical.

## Common misconceptions

- **"A better prompt will stop it hallucinating."** Prompting can reduce the rate and steer
  behaviour, but there is no incantation that makes a plausibility engine truthful. The
  control is architectural (grounding + verification + review), not lexical.
- **"RAG fixes hallucination."** Grounding *bounds* it — the model answers from retrieved
  text instead of memory — but it can still mis-read, over-generalise, or cite a source
  that doesn't actually support the claim (exactly the trap in the demo). RAG lowers the
  rate; it does not guarantee truth.
- **"Temperature 0 makes it accurate."** Lower [temperature](/concepts/sampling) makes
  output more *deterministic*, not more *correct* — it can just as easily lock in a
  confident wrong answer.
- **"If it cites a source, it's verified."** A citation that exists is not a citation that
  supports the claim. Resolution is necessary, not sufficient.
- **"Hallucination means the model is broken."** It's working as trained. The model is a
  fluent text predictor doing exactly that; truth was never in its objective.

## Build it, then own it

The L1 reliability build project behind this page is a real, deterministic control:
[`src/lib/reliability/citation-check.ts`](https://github.com/ZeroCool989/agent-atlas/blob/main/src/lib/reliability/citation-check.ts)
— it parses `[source-id]` citations, flags any that were never retrieved (fabricated) and
any claim made with no citation, and returns a per-sentence report. Read it end to end and
notice what it deliberately does *not* attempt: it never claims a resolving citation
*supports* its sentence. That honest boundary — easy to verify existence, hard to verify
support — is the whole concept in one small, testable file. The interview questions below
test exactly that judgment.

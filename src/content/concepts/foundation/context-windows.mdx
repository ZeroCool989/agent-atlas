## What problem existed before this?

There was no "before": every transformer has had a bounded attention span. The problem
is older than the name — any system that reasons over text needs a working set, and
hardware and attention costs bound how large that set can be.

## Why was this invented?

The context window isn't a feature; it's a budget imposed by how attention scales with
sequence length. Vendors expose it as a hard limit so callers can plan around it rather
than discover it by having requests fail or content silently dropped.

## How does it work?

Every token of your system prompt, conversation history, retrieved documents, and the
model's own output-in-progress occupies a slot. When the budget is spent, nothing more
fits — something must be dropped, summarized, or retrieved on demand.

## Is it actually necessary?

Understanding it is — the limit binds every LLM application. You cannot opt out; you can
only decide *deliberately* what deserves the budget instead of letting truncation decide
for you.

## What are the trade-offs?

Bigger windows fit more but cost more per call and can degrade attention quality over
very long inputs ("lost in the middle"). Smaller windows are cheap and focused but force
aggressive selection of what the model sees.

## What complexity does it introduce?

Token budgeting, truncation strategies, summarization of history, and retrieval
pipelines all exist mainly to serve this limit. Multi-turn products need an explicit
policy for what falls out of the window and when.

## What simpler alternative exists?

For one-shot tasks with small inputs: nothing — just stay under the limit. Long-context
models can replace retrieval machinery for medium corpora, at a per-call price.

## When should I use it?

Always: budget your context deliberately. Reserve room for the answer, keep the system
prompt lean, and measure real token counts rather than guessing from characters.

## When should I avoid it?

Avoid *stuffing* it: dumping whole documents into context because they fit. Past a
point, retrieval of the relevant slice beats brute-force inclusion on cost, latency,
and answer quality.

## What problem existed before this?

Base language models completed text; they didn't follow instructions. Getting a specific
behavior out of them meant shaping the prompt so the desired behavior was the natural
continuation.

## Why was this invented?

GPT-3 era work showed models could "learn" a task from a handful of examples placed in
the prompt — in-context learning — without any weight updates. Few-shot prompting
operationalized that observation.

## How does it work?

You prepend input→output example pairs before the real input. The examples occupy
tokens like any other text — this is prompt content, not training:

## Is it actually necessary?

Less than it used to be. Instruction-tuned models follow well-written zero-shot
instructions for most common tasks; examples still earn their tokens for unusual
formats and fine-grained edge-case behavior.

## What are the trade-offs?

Examples buy precision at the price of tokens on every call, and they anchor the model:
biased or unrepresentative examples reproduce their bias in outputs.

## What complexity does it introduce?

Example selection becomes a maintained artifact — someone must curate, version, and
re-evaluate the examples as models and requirements change.

## What simpler alternative exists?

Zero-shot with an explicit output schema (structured output) covers many cases that
once needed examples.

## When should I use it?

Unusual output formats, subtle classification boundaries, or style imitation — cases
where showing beats telling.

## When should I avoid it?

When a clear instruction already works (measure it), or when context budget is tight
and the examples crowd out task-relevant content.

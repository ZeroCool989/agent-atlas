A [tool-using agent](/concepts/workflows-vs-agents) is one loop: one model, one context window, one
set of tools, deciding a step at a time. A **multi-agent system** is a *composition* of several such
agents — each with its own role, tools, and context — coordinated by an **orchestrator**. It sits at
the very top of the autonomy spectrum: not a new capability, but an arrangement of all the lower
tiers, and with the composition comes both the highest capability *and* the highest cost and risk.

> **Mental model:** a multi-agent system is an **org chart, not a genius**. A manager (the
> orchestrator) splits a job across specialists and stitches their answers together. Where the
> analogy breaks: an org chart also adds *meetings* — coordination, miscommunication, and payroll —
> and a bigger org is not automatically smarter. Most of the time the honest move is to hire one
> good generalist, not to reorganize.

## What problem existed before this?

A single agent has hard limits. Its context window is finite, so a task that needs a mountain of
material — a whole codebase, a long research corpus — overflows it. Its prompt can only carry so many
instructions before "be a careful researcher AND a concise writer AND a skeptical fact-checker"
starts pulling the model in different directions. And an agent asked to check its own work shares its
own blind spots: self-review is not independent verification (see [reflection](/concepts/reflection)).
On long or multi-skill tasks, one greedy loop drifts, forgets, or confidently ships its own mistakes.

## Why was this invented?

To buy four things by *splitting* the work: **context isolation** (each agent sees only what its
subtask needs, so no single window has to hold everything), **specialization** (a focused role with a
focused prompt and a focused toolset beats one overloaded generalist), **parallelism** (independent
subtasks can run at once), and **separation of concerns** — the load-bearing one — so that a
*different* agent, with a different job, can check the first one's work. The recurring shapes:

- **Supervisor / worker** — a coordinator decomposes the goal and delegates subtasks to specialist
  workers, then composes their results. (The pattern the visual below runs.)
- **Planner / executor** — builds directly on [planning](/concepts/planning): a planner decomposes
  the task into steps; executors carry them out; the planner re-plans when a step fails.
- **Debate / critic** — agents check each other. One proposes, another critiques or verifies; this is
  [reflection](/concepts/reflection) made *independent* by giving the critic a separate context.
- **Pipeline** — a staged hand-off, each stage transforming the previous stage's output (research →
  draft → verify).

These are compositions, not magic: every worker is still just a tool-using agent, and the orchestrator
is still just a loop.

## How does it work?

An orchestrator decomposes the goal into subtasks and **delegates** each to a role-specialized worker
— a hand-off that is literally a tool call, where the "tool" is another agent (*agents-as-tools*). The
worker runs, returns a result, and the orchestrator collects it, forwards it as context to whoever
needs it next, re-delegates around failures, and finally composes an answer — all under hard caps so
it can never loop forever. Watch a supervisor build a vetted brief: it delegates research, then a
draft, then verification; the **independent critic catches a claim the writer invented**; the
supervisor re-delegates a fix; only then is the answer composed. The final beat sets the whole run's
cost against a single good agent on the same work:

The mechanism is the deterministic orchestrator in this repo (`src/lib/multi-agent/`): a supervisor

the message-passing shape is unchanged — that substitution is the whole point (ADR-0005).

## Is it actually necessary?

**Usually not.** This is the load-bearing honesty of the whole concept: *most tasks that look
multi-agent are better solved by one well-structured agent or a deterministic workflow.* If you can
write the decomposition down in advance, you don't need agents coordinating at runtime — you need a
workflow (you did the planning, once, in code). If one agent with the right tools and a tight step
limit can do it, a system of agents is just more moving parts, more calls, and more ways to fail.
Multi-agent earns its keep only when the task genuinely exceeds one agent — too much context,
genuinely distinct skills, or a real need for *independent* checking — and you can afford to evaluate
and operate the whole system. Treat "let's use multiple agents" as a claim to justify, never a default.

## What are the trade-offs?

You buy capability on hard tasks and pay in cost, reliability, and observability. **Cost multiplies**:
every agent is more model calls — the demo above spends nine calls to catch one mistake a single
agent would have made in one call. You inherit **new failure modes that don't exist in one loop**:
agents talking past each other, a wrong result from one worker **cascading** through everyone
downstream, and **deadlock or looping** when agents wait on or re-delegate to each other without
bound (see [failure modes](/concepts/failure-modes)). And you can no longer just evaluate a model:
you must [evaluate](/concepts/evaluation) a *system* whose behavior emerges from the interaction —
much harder to test, reproduce, and debug, because the bug is often in the *hand-off*, not any one
agent.

## What complexity does it introduce?

An orchestration layer (decomposition, routing, retries, caps, composition); message-passing and
state that must be represented, persisted, and inspected; and a debugging surface that is now a
*distributed system* — you trace failures across agents and messages, not down one call stack. Every
worker carries the full tool-using agent's burden (traces, limits, evals) and the orchestrator adds
its own on top. This is precisely why multi-agent lives in the `advanced-system` layer: it composes
everything below it, so it inherits every lower cost and adds coordination cost that nothing lower has.

## What simpler alternative exists?

In order of preference: a **deterministic workflow** (developer-authored steps, no runtime discretion
— testable, cheap, predictable); a **single well-structured agent** with good tools and a tight step
limit; a single agent plus a **planning** phase for genuinely contingent tasks; or, if you only need a
second opinion, in-loop [reflection/verification](/concepts/verification) rather than a whole second
agent. Reach for multiple agents only when those genuinely can't express the task — and remember that
"add another agent" is often reaching for architecture to paper over an unclear task or a wrong model
choice.

## When should I use it?

When the task truly exceeds one agent and the split is real: a context too large for one window
(so workers each own a slice); genuinely different skills that conflict in one prompt (research vs.
writing vs. adversarial checking); independent subtasks worth parallelizing; and — the strongest case
— when you need **independent verification** that a self-reviewing agent structurally can't give, so a
separate critic with its own context earns its cost. And only when you can afford to evaluate and
supervise the whole system, because you are now operating a distributed one.

## When should I avoid it?

When a workflow or a single good agent already solves it (most of the time); when the "subtasks" are
really one coherent task you've split for aesthetic reasons; when you can't afford the multiplied cost
and latency, or can't evaluate a system-level behavior; and any time multi-agent is reached for to
look sophisticated — *architecture-astronomy*. A system of agents makes a confused design
*confidently* wrong across several models at once, and bills you for each. If you can't state what
each agent gives you that one agent couldn't, you don't have a multi-agent problem.

## Common misconceptions

- **"More agents means more intelligence."** No — it means more coordination. Capability comes from a
  split that matches the task's real structure; a bad split adds cost and failure modes with no gain.
- **"A critic agent guarantees correctness."** Only if it is genuinely independent and actually better
  at judging than at doing. A critic that shares the writer's model and blind spots reproduces
  [reflection](/concepts/reflection)'s sycophancy trap at higher cost.
- **"Multi-agent is a higher, better tier you graduate to."** It is the top of the autonomy spectrum
  because it *composes* the lower tiers — which means it inherits all their risks and adds new ones.
  Higher on the spectrum is more capable *and* more dangerous, not simply better.
- **"You can evaluate it like a single agent."** You can't. Emergent, interaction-level behavior needs
  system-level evaluation — end-to-end tests, per-hand-off checks, and traces across agents.

## Autonomy, oversight, and risk

Multi-agent systems sit at the top of the autonomy spectrum, so they concentrate the exact risks
governance frameworks care about: **compounding autonomy** (many steps and many agents between human
decisions), **cascading errors**, and **diffuse accountability** — when several agents contribute to a
harmful output, *who is responsible?* The [EU AI Act](/governance/eu-ai-act)'s human-oversight and
record-keeping expectations for higher-risk systems push toward inspectable orchestration: a logged
decomposition, per-hand-off traces, checkpoints a human can interrupt, and bounded delegation rather
than fire-and-forget. The [NIST AI RMF](/governance/nist-ai-rmf)'s map–measure–manage framing lands
hard here, because you must measure a *system*, not a model. The honest engineering point and the
governance point coincide: a multi-agent system you cannot **audit, evaluate, and bound** is one you
should not ship — and the discipline that makes it governable (traces, caps, system-level evals) is
the same discipline that makes it work at all. Applicability always depends on the use case, sector,
and jurisdiction.

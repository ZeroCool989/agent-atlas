An agent run is a nondeterministic, multi-step, tool-using process: the model decides,
the runtime executes, a tool returns something, the model decides again. When that run
misbehaves in production — a wrong answer, a slow response, a spike in the bill — the
question you must answer is *what happened, and why?* **Observability** is instrumenting
the run so you can. It is the operational counterpart to
[verification](/concepts/verification): verification blocks a bad output *before* you act
on it; observability lets you *see* the whole run afterward, so you can debug it, watch
its cost and latency, and catch a regression the day it starts.

> **Mental model: a flight recorder for the agent loop.** Instead of one final error
> message, you record a nested trace of every span — each model call, tool call, retry and
> decision — with its own duration, tokens and cost, so you can replay exactly where the
> time and the mistake went. **Where the analogy breaks:** a flight recorder captures
> physical facts; a trace captures *observable behavior only*. It shows what each step did,
> not the model's private reasoning for doing it — the trace tells you *what*, and often
> narrows the *why*, but the model's intent stays opaque.

## What problem existed before this?

For ordinary software, a stack trace is enough: deterministic code fails at a line, and
the line explains it. An agent breaks that. The run is a *sequence of model decisions and
tool results*, and the failure is usually not in your code at all — it's a model that
picked the wrong tool, a tool that returned stale data, a hand-off between agents that
dropped context, or a retry that quietly masked a flaky dependency. Re-running rarely
reproduces it, because the model is nondeterministic. You are left with "it was wrong that
one time" and no way to see the step where it went wrong — and separately, no idea why last
month's bill doubled or why p95 latency crept up, because nothing recorded per-step cost or
duration.

## Why was this invented?

Because you cannot operate or debug what you cannot see, and agents made the blindness
acute. The fix is to borrow the discipline that already runs distributed systems — the
**three pillars of observability**, traces, logs and metrics — and adapt them to LLM
systems. Record every step as a span in a tree, log the inputs and outputs and token/cost
figures, and aggregate the whole into metrics you can alert on. Do that and "sometimes
wrong in production" stops being a shrug and becomes a specific span you can open, read,
and fix.

## How does it work?

Three pillars, adapted to an agent run:

- **Tracing** — the load-bearing one. You wrap each unit of work in a **span**:
  `startSpan` when it begins, `endSpan` when it ends. Because spans nest, the agent loop
  becomes a *tree* — a root run span with a model call, a tool call, a retry and a
  response beneath it, each with a start, an end, and its own measurements. The tree is the
  shape of the run.
- **Logging** — the detail inside each span: the model's inputs and outputs, the tool
  arguments and results, and the tokens, cost and latency of that step. Structured
  (key–value), not `console.log`, so you can query it.
- **Metrics** — the spans rolled up into aggregates you watch continuously: total cost and
  latency, error and failure rates, the slowest span, tool-usage counts. Metrics are what
  you put on a dashboard and alert on.

bug both live in a tool result, visible only in the trace.

The fourth part is what makes this *ongoing* rather than a one-time debug: **online
evaluation and drift detection**. [Evaluation](/concepts/evaluation) starts offline — a
fixed suite you run before shipping. Observability is where it moves *online*: you sample
real production traces, score them (thumbs, task success, an LLM judge), and watch that
score over time. When it slides — because traffic drifted from your eval set, or a provider
changed a model under you — the trend is the early warning. Offline evals catch regressions
you cause; online signals catch the ones the world causes.

## Is it actually necessary?

For anything you *operate* — a running system with real users, cost, and a pager — yes, at
some level: without instrumentation, a production failure is undiagnosable and a cost or
latency regression is invisible until someone complains. But "necessary" is a spectrum, not
a platform. The minimum that earns its keep is small: structured logging on each step and a
single cost/latency metric. You genuinely do not need a distributed-tracing platform to run
a modest feature — and standing one up before you have anything in production is premature.
Necessity scales with how much the system matters and how many steps it takes to fail.

## What are the trade-offs?

You gain the ability to answer "what happened and why," and pay in three currencies.
**Instrumentation**: spans and structured logs are code you write and maintain, and an
un-instrumented step is a blind spot exactly where you'll need to see. **Overhead**:
telemetry costs storage, a little latency, and money — traces of verbose model I/O add up
fast, so you sample. **A governance surface**: logging inputs and outputs means your logs
now contain whatever users typed and the model returned — PII, secrets, regulated data —
which turns retention, access and erasure into obligations you didn't have before. The
honest tension is that the richest trace is also the riskiest log.

## What complexity does it introduce?

Real machinery once you pass the minimum: a span model and the discipline to open and close
spans around every step (including inside retries and hand-offs); a place to store traces
and logs, with sampling so cost stays bounded; a metrics pipeline and the dashboards and
alert thresholds on top of it; and, if you do online evaluation, a scorer running over live
traffic — a *second* evaluation problem, since an LLM judge must itself be validated.
Adopting a managed tracing platform trades writing that for configuring and paying for it,
plus sending your prompt and output data to a third party — its own governance question.

## What simpler alternative exists?

Structured logging plus one metric. Log each step as key–value data — the step name,
inputs, outputs, tokens, latency, and an error field — and emit a single cost-and-latency
number you can chart. That is queryable, greppable telemetry that answers most "what
happened" questions and catches most cost/latency regressions, for an afternoon of work and
almost no new infrastructure. Reach for full nested tracing when the *structure* of the run
is the thing you can't see from flat logs — deep tool chains, multi-agent hand-offs, retries
you need to correlate — and for a managed platform only when operating the system justifies
the overhead. A tracing stack nobody reads is worse than five good log lines and one alert.

## When should I use it?

When you *operate* an agent: real users, real cost, a failure that matters. Add the minimum
— structured step logs and a cost/latency metric — the moment something ships, and add
tracing when a run has enough steps or hand-offs that a flat log can't show you the shape of
a failure. Reach for online evaluation when offline evals no longer reflect production, and
for a full platform when the number of services, the incident rate, or a compliance
requirement for audit trails justifies it.

## When should I avoid it?

Don't build a tracing platform before you have something in production to trace — that's
infrastructure for a problem you don't have yet. For a deterministic workflow with no model
in the loop, ordinary application logging already suffices; the agent-specific machinery
earns its place only when nondeterminism and multi-step tool use are what make failures
opaque. And never instrument blindly: piping raw inputs and outputs into logs "just in case"
is how PII and secrets end up in a system with the wrong retention and access controls —
decide what you capture, redact, and keep *before* you turn it on, not after an audit.

## Observability vs evaluation vs verification

These three are the reliability trio and interviews probe the boundaries, so keep them
crisp:

- **Verification** blocks a *single* bad output *inline, before you act* — deterministic
  gates, synchronous, a yes/no on this one output (see
  [verification](/concepts/verification)).
- **Evaluation** measures *quality* over a *set* of cases — is the system good? — mostly
  offline, producing a score (see [evaluation](/concepts/evaluation)).
- **Observability** lets you *operate and debug the live system* — what happened and why,
  across real runs — after the fact, producing traces and metrics.

They interlock: observability's online signals feed evaluation; a verification failure is an
event worth tracing; and the reliable-agent tier of the autonomy spectrum needs all three.
Different jobs, different timing — don't let an interviewer collapse them.

## Common misconceptions

- **"Observability is just logging."** Logging is one pillar. The trace *tree* and the
  rolled-up *metrics* are what turn scattered log lines into the shape of a run and a
  dashboard you can alert on.
- **"A green trace means it worked."** A trace shows *what happened*, not that the answer
  was correct — that's evaluation's job. `completed` is an outcome, not a correctness proof.
- **"The trace tells you why."** It tells you *what*, and often narrows the why to a
  specific span — but the model's reasoning is opaque, so a trace localizes a failure
  without always explaining the model's intent.
- **"More telemetry is always better."** Verbose traces cost storage, latency and money, and
  every logged input is a PII/secrets liability. You sample and redact on purpose.
- **"Observability replaces verification."** It's after-the-fact; it never blocks a bad
  output inline. You need both.

## Security and governance

- **Traces and logs are the audit trail.** A dated, per-step record of what the system did —
  inputs, decisions, tool calls, outputs — is exactly the record-keeping and monitoring
  evidence that governance processes ask for. It's the concrete artifact behind "we can
  explain and account for what our AI did."
- **Regulatory frame, carefully.** Record-keeping and post-market monitoring expectations for
  high-risk systems in the EU AI Act, and the continual-monitoring emphasis in the NIST AI
  RMF, are satisfied *in practice* by traces, logs and metrics and their retention — but none
  of these apply *merely because* a system is observable; applicability depends on the system,
  its use case, and jurisdiction.
- **Logs are a data-protection liability, not just a debugging aid.** The moment you log
  inputs and outputs, your logs may hold personal data — which brings GDPR obligations for
  lawful basis, retention limits, access control, and the right to erasure. "We keep full
  traces forever" is a finding waiting to happen. Redact or pseudonymize PII at capture, set
  retention deliberately, and be able to delete a subject's data from your telemetry.

## Build it, then own it

The tracer is the L5-style build project and a few dozen readable lines:
[`tracer.ts`](https://github.com/ZeroCool989/agent-atlas/blob/main/src/lib/observability/tracer.ts)
— a `Tracer` with `startSpan`/`endSpan` over an injected clock, and a pure `buildTrace` that
nests the spans, computes each span's self (exclusive) duration, rolls tokens and cost up to
the root, and derives the metrics (total cost, error rate, slowest span, retry count).
`buildDemoTrace` is the exact run the visual above shows. Read it, notice how the slowest
span and the errored span turn out to be the same one, then try the interview questions below.

Everything upstream in the [autonomy spectrum](/concepts/workflows-vs-agents) assumed the call
*worked*. In production it often doesn't: a model times out, a provider returns a 503 or a rate
limit, the output comes back malformed, a tool returns a bad result. These are not bugs to be
patched away — they are the [structural reality](/concepts/failure-modes) that models and tools fail
*probabilistically*. **Reliability patterns** are the classic distributed-systems techniques —
retries with backoff and jitter, timeouts and budgets, fallbacks, and circuit breakers — adapted to
LLM apps so the system stays usable *despite* that. They are the retries-and-fallbacks half of what
makes a [reliable agent](/concepts/workflows-vs-agents): a stateful agent hardened with verification,
retries, guardrails, evals, and observability.

> **Mental model: an experienced traveller with a backup plan.** They don't assume the train runs on
> time — they leave a buffer (a timeout), try again if a connection is missed (a bounded retry), keep
> a slower route in their pocket (a fallback), and if a whole line is clearly down they stop queuing
> for it and reroute (a circuit breaker). The trip still happens.
> **Where the analogy breaks:** a traveller's retry is free and side-effect-free. In software a retry
> costs money and latency, can *repeat an action* (re-charge a card, re-send a message), and can
> quietly paper over a real failure — so the discipline is knowing when *not* to retry, which the
> traveller never has to think about.

## What problem existed before this?

Earlier lessons handled *wrong* output; none handled *failed or slow* calls. [Failure
modes](/concepts/failure-modes) showed the model can be confidently wrong; [verification](/concepts/verification)
showed how to check an output before trusting it. But verification assumes you *got* an output. The
gap is everything that happens when you don't: the provider is rate-limited, the request hangs, the
tool call returns an error, the network blips. A naive agent loop treats these as fatal — one
transient 503 ends the run, or worse, an un-bounded call hangs forever and takes a worker with it.
The [minimal agent loop in this repo](/concepts/tool-calling) is deliberately honest about this: a
tool error *ends the run*, with a comment that feeding the error back for a retry "belongs to the
reliability concept." This is that concept.

## Why was this invented?

Because reliability is a property you *build around* an unreliable dependency, and distributed
systems solved this decades before LLMs existed. A remote call over a network has always been able
to be slow, fail transiently, or fail durably — so the field developed a small, composable toolkit:
**retry** a transient failure (with **backoff** so you don't hammer a struggling service, and
**jitter** so a swarm of clients doesn't retry in lockstep); **timeout** every call so a hang becomes
a fast, handleable failure; **fall back** to a cheaper or cached answer instead of failing hard; and
trip a **circuit breaker** to stop calling a dependency that is clearly down. LLM apps inherit all of
it, plus two twists of their own — a retry with the *same prompt* can fail the same way (so you
sometimes retry with a *repair* prompt), and a retried *tool call* can repeat a real-world action (so
retries are only safe on idempotent operations).

## How does it work?

Each pattern is a small wrapper around a call. Step a single request through the whole stack — a
transient failure ridden out by backoff+retry, a slow call that times out and degrades to a cheaper
model, and a dead dependency that trips a breaker and then recovers:

Every attempt count, backoff delay, timeout, and breaker transition is computed by the [resilience
module in this repo](https://github.com/ZeroCool989/agent-atlas/tree/main/src/lib/resilience) — the
operations are illustrative, the wrappers are the real thing. The four patterns:

- **Retry with backoff + jitter** (`withRetry`). Re-attempt a *transient* failure, bounded by a max
  attempt count. The delay grows exponentially (`base · factor^n`, capped) so you back off a
  struggling service, and **jitter** spreads the actual delay randomly inside that ceiling so many
  clients don't reconnect in synchronised waves. A **retry predicate** decides what's worth retrying
  — a 503, yes; a 400, no. The LLM twist: the visual's winning attempt sends a *repair prompt*, not
  the same prompt again, because an identical retry of a deterministic failure fails identically.
- **Timeout + budget** (`withTimeout`). Give every call a deadline; if it passes, abort the *wait*
  and raise a fast failure instead of hanging. Bound the *total* budget across retries too, or three
  retries silently triple your worst-case latency and cost. (Honest caveat: a JavaScript timeout
  bounds how long you *wait*, not how long the dependency *works* — real cancellation needs an
  `AbortSignal` the call honours.)
- **Fallback** (`withFallback`). When the primary fails, serve something else — a cheaper/smaller
  model, a cached answer, or a partial result. **Graceful degradation**: a worse answer beats a hard
  failure — *if* you can see how often it fires.
- **Circuit breaker** (`CircuitBreaker`). When a dependency is *durably* down, retries make it worse.
  The breaker counts consecutive failures; at a threshold it **opens** and fast-fails calls *without
  touching the dependency*; after a cooldown it goes **half-open** and lets one trial through; a
  success **closes** it. This is the difference from a retry: a retry rides out a *per-request blip*;
  a breaker protects the *whole system* so retries don't turn an outage into a self-inflicted DDoS.

These compose: a real call path is a breaker wrapping a fallback wrapping a retry wrapping a timeout.
And they tie back to the rest of the stack — a failed [verification](/concepts/verification) check is
one of the things that *triggers* a retry or fallback, and you need [observability](/concepts/observability)
traces to know what was retried and how often the fallback fired.

## Is it actually necessary?

Proportional to stakes — this is the honest core. A one-shot script or a low-stakes internal tool
needs almost nothing: maybe a timeout so it can't hang. A user-facing agent that makes real calls all
day needs bounded retries and timeouts as table stakes, and fallbacks and a breaker in proportion to
what an outage costs. You never add a circuit breaker to something that runs once — reliability
engineering is a cost you pay against a risk, and if the risk is trivial the engineering is waste.
The question to ask at each pattern is the same as everywhere on the atlas: *what failure does this
prevent, and does the value clear its cost?*

## What are the trade-offs?

- **Retries cost money and latency.** Every retry is another paid call and more wall-clock time; a
  per-request budget matters as much as the per-attempt timeout.
- **Retries can mask a real bug.** A retry only fixes a *transient* failure. Retrying a deterministic
  one — a malformed prompt, a schema mismatch, a 400 — pays 2–3× for the same failure and hides it: a
  green success rate over a rising true error rate. A healthy retry profile is mostly first-attempt
  successes; a heavy tail of retries is a bug report.
- **Retries can duplicate side effects.** Re-running a non-idempotent action (charge, send, insert, a
  tool that acts on the world) repeats it. The fix is **idempotency** — an idempotency key the
  downstream honours — not a blind retry. This is the sharpest hazard for tool-using agents.
- **Fallbacks can silently degrade quality.** "Always return something" can turn a hard failure into a
  soft, unlogged *worse* answer. Availability is not correctness. The fallback rate must be a
  first-class, alertable metric or quality rots while every request still returns 200.
- **A breaker is a tuning problem.** Too sensitive and a blip opens it and needlessly degrades
  everyone; too lax and it never protects anything.

## What complexity does it introduce?

New control flow that must itself be correct and bounded: a retry loop that always terminates, a
timeout that cleans up, a breaker with three states and a cooldown, and the composition order among
them (breaker outermost, timeout innermost). New parameters to choose from real data — attempt
counts, base delay and cap, jitter mode, failure threshold, cooldown — none of which have universal
right answers. New failure modes of your *own* making — retry storms, thundering herds, duplicated
actions, silent degradation — that only exist because you added the patterns. And a hard dependency
on [observability](/concepts/observability): a reliability layer you can't *see* (retry rate,
fallback rate, breaker state) hides more than it fixes. "We added retries" quietly means "we own a
bounded loop, a set of tuned parameters, an idempotency story, and the metrics to prove it helps."

## What simpler alternative exists?

In order: **make the call not fail** — fix the deterministic bug a retry would have masked, since a
retry is a painkiller, not a cure. Then the **80/20**: one timeout plus a small bounded retry on
transient errors only. That removes hangs and rides out blips for almost no complexity. Reach for a
fallback only when you have a genuinely-better-than-failing degraded path *and* you'll monitor it;
reach for a circuit breaker only when a dependency's durable outages are actually hurting you. And
prefer boring, readable wrappers over a resilience *framework* — the four patterns are a few dozen
lines each; an elaborate policy engine nobody understands is worse than a timeout and a retry you can
read.

## When should I use it?

When a call can fail or be slow and that failure would cost something: any user-facing agent, any
loop that makes many model or tool calls, anything with a latency or availability target. Start with
timeouts and bounded retries on transient errors everywhere; add fallbacks where a degraded answer is
genuinely better than an error *and* observable; add a circuit breaker around a dependency whose
outages you've actually felt. Always retry with backoff *and* jitter, always bound the total budget,
and always retry side-effecting actions only behind an idempotency key. And log every timeout, retry,
fallback, and breaker transition — that log is your debugging surface and your evidence that the layer
helps.

## When should I avoid it?

When the call can't meaningfully fail, or the failure is free — a run-once script, a local
deterministic step, a low-stakes internal tool. Don't add a circuit breaker to something that runs
once; don't retry a non-idempotent action without an idempotency key; don't retry a deterministic
error (a 400, a validation failure) — it just burns money and hides the bug. Don't let a fallback
degrade *silently*, and for a consequential decision, prefer to **fail safe** — return an error or
hand off to a human — rather than degrade into a confidently wrong answer to hit an availability
number. If you can't *observe* the reliability layer, assume it's masking something.

## Common misconceptions

- **"Retries make the system reliable."** Retries make *transient* failures survivable. Against a
  deterministic bug they amortise the same failure and hide it; against a durable outage, without
  jitter and a breaker, they *amplify* it. Reliability is the whole set used with judgment, not retries
  alone.
- **"A retry is free."** It costs a paid call, latency, and — for a side-effecting operation — a risk
  of doing the action twice. Retries are only safe on idempotent operations.
- **"Retrying the same LLM prompt will eventually work."** If the prompt is what's wrong, an identical
  retry fails identically. The fix is a repair prompt, or a different model — not another blind try.
- **"A fallback firing means we're fine."** It means you're *up*, on a worse answer. If you're not
  tracking the fallback rate, quality can rot while every request still returns 200.
- **"A circuit breaker is just a fancy retry."** Opposite jobs: a retry *keeps trying* to rescue one
  request; a breaker *stops trying* to protect the system so a struggling dependency can recover.

## Autonomy, oversight, and risk

Reliability patterns are how you keep an autonomous system *available and controlled* under failure,
which is why they meet governance where robustness and operational resilience are expected — but they
also introduce a governance-specific risk. **Graceful degradation is a feature until it degrades
silently.** A fallback that serves a lower-quality answer is acceptable only when it is observable and
the residual risk is understood; serving a *wrong* answer to hit an availability target is the
anti-pattern, because availability is not correctness. For consequential decisions the governed choice
is often to **fail safe** — an honest error or a human handoff — rather than degrade into a confidently
wrong output. The auditable evidence is the resilience design *plus* the logs of what timed out, what
was retried, when a fallback fired, and when a breaker opened; the same trace that lets you debug is
what shows a system behaved in a controlled way. This connects to the [EU AI Act](/governance/eu-ai-act)'s
accuracy-and-robustness expectations for high-risk systems and the "manage" function of the
[NIST AI RMF](/governance/nist-ai-rmf) (and to operational-resilience regimes like DORA for regulated
firms). None of them applies *merely because* a system has retries — applicability depends on the
system, role, use case, data, and jurisdiction — and the honest caveat governance most needs to hear
is that retries can *mask* a rising failure rate, so the retry and fallback rates belong in monitoring,
not just the happy-path SLA.

## Build it, then own it

The resilience module is small, dependency-free, unit-tested, and deterministic — four composable
wrappers you can read in one sitting:
[`patterns.ts`](https://github.com/ZeroCool989/agent-atlas/blob/main/src/lib/resilience/patterns.ts)
holds `withRetry` (backoff + jitter, a retry predicate, an injected clock), `withTimeout`,
`withFallback`, and a three-state `CircuitBreaker`, all driven by an injected `Clock` and `Rng` so a
`ManualClock` replays them in exact virtual time with no real waiting. The three fixtures in
[`demo.ts`](https://github.com/ZeroCool989/agent-atlas/blob/main/src/lib/resilience/demo.ts) are the
lesson in code: a flaky call recovered on a *repair-prompt* retry after two backoffs, a slow primary
that times out at 2000ms and degrades to a cheaper model at 2300ms, and a dependency that trips the
breaker after three failures, fast-fails the next calls, and closes again on a healthy trial. Read
`withRetry` and notice the honesty the types force: the operation takes an `attempt` number (so it can
repair, not just repeat), and nothing here makes a side effect safe to retry — that's *your*
idempotency key, not the wrapper's job. Then try the interview questions below.

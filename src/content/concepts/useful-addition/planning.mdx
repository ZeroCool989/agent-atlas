A tool-using agent decides one step at a time (see [workflows vs agents](/concepts/workflows-vs-agents)). That
is enough for short tasks, but on longer ones a purely greedy loop drifts — it optimizes the
next move without a picture of the whole job. **Planning** adds a phase before acting: the agent
produces a multi-step plan, executes it, and revises the plan when reality pushes back. It is a
*technique inside* the tool-using / stateful agent, not a new rung on the autonomy ladder.

## What problem existed before this?

A step-at-a-time agent chooses each action from the current messages and tools alone. For a task
whose later steps depend on getting earlier ones right — book the room *after* you know who's
free, *after* you know the team size — greedy selection has no representation of "the whole job."
It can wander, redo work, or commit to an action it can't take back, and it has no artifact to
point at when a human asks "what is it going to do?"

## Why was this invented?

To give the agent an explicit, inspectable **plan** — an ordered set of intended steps produced
before execution — so the system reasons about the task as a whole, can be checked before it acts,
and can *revise* course when a step fails instead of blundering onward. Patterns range from
**plan-then-execute** (decompose the task up front, then run the steps) to **ReAct**-style
interleaving (reason → act → observe, re-deciding each turn) to explicit **task decomposition**
into subtasks. They share one idea: separate *deciding what to do* from *doing it*, and keep the
decision visible.

## How does it work?

Plan-then-execute is the clearest version. A planner turns the goal into ordered steps; a runtime
executes them; when a step fails, the planner is called again to **re-plan the remainder** around
the failure. Watch it run — a plan is made up front, the 9:00 room check fails, and the planner
routes to the next open slot rather than insisting on 9:00. The final beat shows the same task run
greedily, with no plan to revise, drifting instead:

The mechanism is the deterministic harness in this repo (`src/lib/planning/`): a `Planner`
(goal → steps), a `StepExecutor` (run one step, report success or a recoverable failure), and a
loop that executes in order and re-plans on failure — bounded by a step cap and a re-plan cap so it
can never loop forever. In production the planner is a model call; here it's a fixed function, which
is the point of ADR-0005 — swap the planner for a model and the loop is unchanged.

## Is it actually necessary?

Usually not. If you can write the steps down in advance, you don't need the model to plan — you need
a **workflow** (you did the planning, in code, once). Planning earns its keep only when the sequence
genuinely can't be known ahead of time and depends on what earlier steps reveal. Treat "we added a
planner" as a claim to justify, not a default.

## What are the trade-offs?

You buy coherence on long tasks and pay in predictability and cost. A plan is an extra model call
(latency, tokens) and a new failure mode: a **confident wrong plan** executed to completion is worse
than a greedy agent that at least fails early. Re-planning helps but can loop; you now need
checkpoints, step and re-plan bounds, and evaluation to know the planner is any good. Autonomy went
up, and so did the amount you must verify.

## What complexity does it introduce?

A plan is state to represent, persist, and reason about; re-planning is control flow that must
terminate; and a plan executed without human checkpoints raises the stakes of every step. You inherit
the tool-using agent's whole burden — traces, limits, evals — and add the plan on top. This is why
planning lives in the `useful-addition` layer: valuable for the right problem, pure overhead for the
wrong one.

## What simpler alternative exists?

In order of preference: a **fixed workflow** (developer-authored steps, no model discretion); a
**single, well-structured prompt** that asks for the answer directly (often the "plan" was imaginary);
or a plain **tool-using agent** with a tight step limit. Add planning only when those genuinely can't
express the task. A fixed sequence you can test beats an agentic plan you have to babysit.

## When should I use it?

When the task is multi-step, the steps depend on runtime discoveries, and the sequence can't be
hard-coded — research-and-synthesize tasks, multi-tool workflows where the branch depends on results,
long jobs where a greedy agent measurably loses the thread. And when you can afford checkpoints: a
plan you can inspect and interrupt is much safer than one that runs to completion unattended.

## When should I avoid it?

When a workflow or a good prompt already solves it (most of the time); when the task is short enough
that greedy step-selection is fine; when you can't afford the extra latency/cost or can't supervise a
multi-step run; and any time "add a planner" is reaching for autonomy to paper over an unclear task
or a wrong model choice. Planning makes a confused agent *confidently* wrong, faster.

## Common misconceptions

- **"Planning makes the agent a higher tier of autonomy."** No — it's a technique used *inside* a
  tool-using or stateful agent. The autonomy spectrum is about *who decides the next step*; planning
  changes *how* the model decides, not that it decides.
- **"A plan means the agent will do the right thing."** A plan is only as good as the model's
  understanding of the task. A wrong plan executed faithfully is a distinct, often costlier, failure
  than a greedy misstep — see [failure modes](/concepts/failure-modes).
- **"Re-planning fixes failures."** It routes around *recoverable* ones. Some failures no plan can
  rescue; a bounded harness ends honestly as failed rather than looping — which is the correct
  behavior, not a bug.

## Autonomy, oversight, and risk

Planning increases the length of the leash: more steps executed per human decision. That is exactly
where governance frameworks focus. The [EU AI Act](/governance/eu-ai-act)'s human-oversight
expectations for higher-risk systems push toward **checkpoints** (a plan a person can review or
interrupt) rather than fire-and-forget execution, and toward **auditability** — an explicit,
logged plan is far easier to review after the fact than a stream of greedy actions. The reliability
answer (verification between steps, re-planning limits, evals of plan quality) is where planning meets
[evaluation](/concepts/evaluation): you cannot trust a planner you have never measured.

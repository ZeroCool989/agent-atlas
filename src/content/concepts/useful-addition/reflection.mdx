A single generation pass commits to whatever the model produced first — and a first draft is
often not its best output (see [what a language model is](/concepts/what-is-a-language-model)).
**Reflection** adds a loop *after* producing: the agent critiques its own output against a goal
or rubric, then revises — bounded, stopping when the critique passes or improvement stalls. It
goes by several names (self-refinement, reflexion, critic-actor loops) and shares one bet: for
many tasks, *judging* a candidate against explicit criteria is easier than *generating* a
perfect one in a single pass. Like [planning](/concepts/planning), it is a *technique inside* a
tool-using or stateful agent, not a new rung on the autonomy ladder.

> **Mental model: a writer with an editor pass.** Producing the draft and red-penning it are
> different jobs, and doing the second on the first buys real quality — you catch what you'd
> have shipped blind. **Where the analogy breaks:** here the editor is the *same mind* as the
> writer. A human editor is a second, independent reader; a self-critic shares the author's
> blind spots, so it can "fix" a correct sentence into a wrong one and feel more sure, not less.

## What problem existed before this?

One-shot generation has no second look. The model emits an answer from the prompt alone, and if
that answer is almost-right — a summary missing the key number, code with an off-by-one, a plan
with a contradiction — nothing in the process notices, because there is no step whose job is to
*check*. You could re-prompt by hand, but that's you doing the reflecting. The gap is a built-in
phase where the system evaluates its own draft and gets a chance to fix it before it ships.

## Why was this invented?

To close the produce-then-ship gap with a produce-then-*critique*-then-revise loop. The insight
is asymmetry: even when a model can't nail a task in one pass, it can often *tell* what's wrong
with a candidate when handed explicit criteria — the same asymmetry that makes grading an essay
easier than writing one. Reflexion and self-refinement operationalize it: generate a draft, have
a critic score it against a goal or rubric and say what's wrong, feed that back, and revise. The
critic doesn't have to be the model, either — a compiler, a test suite, or a search result can
play it, which is where reflection is strongest.

## How does it work?

Produce → critique → revise, repeated under a bound. A critic scores the current draft against
criteria and returns *what's wrong*; the producer revises using that feedback; the loop stops
when the critic passes or when revisions stop improving the score. Watch it run — a weak first
draft is scored, revised (including one revision that overcorrects and scores *worse*, which the
bounded loop keeps out of), and finally accepted; a last beat shows the honest limit:

The mechanism is the deterministic harness in this repo (`src/lib/reflection/`): a `Critic`
(draft → score + issues), a `Reviser` (goal + draft + critique → revised draft), and a loop
bounded by an iteration cap and a *stall* check that returns the best-scoring draft, not the
last. The load-bearing detail: **the critic is an [evaluation](/concepts/evaluation) applied
inline** — `rubricCritic` is built from the eval harness's own `scoreAssertion`
(`src/lib/eval/harness.ts`), pointed at the draft. Reflection *is* the eval harness, in a loop.
In production the producer and critic are model calls; here they're fixed functions, which is
the point of ADR-0005 — swap them for a model and the loop is unchanged.

Three variants trade off along one axis, **how independent the critic is**:

- **Self-critique** — the model grades its own output. Cheapest, and the *least* independent.
- **External-tool / verifier feedback** — a compiler, test runner, schema, or search result
  grades it. More trustworthy, because the signal comes from outside the model.
- **Multi-sample + select** — generate several candidates and let the critic pick the best
  (`bestOfN` in the module). Parallel instead of iterative, same "judging is easier" bet.

## Is it actually necessary?

Usually not as a first move. If a sharper prompt gets the first draft right, you don't need a
loop; if "correct" is mechanically checkable, a deterministic verifier is simpler and more
reliable than asking the model to re-grade itself. Reflection earns its keep on tasks where the
first pass is reliably imperfect *and* judging a candidate is genuinely easier than generating
one — and even then, only when the critic is trustworthy or externally grounded. Treat "we added
a self-critique step" as a claim to justify with a measurement, not a default.

## What are the trade-offs?

You buy quality on hard-to-one-shot tasks and pay in three currencies. **Cost and latency**:
every iteration is another model call or two, and past the first pass or two the score usually
plateaus — diminishing returns you're still paying for. **A critic that can be wrong**: this is
the honest core. A model judging itself shares its own blind spots, so it can confidently
"improve" a good answer into a worse one, or rubber-stamp a bad one — **sycophancy**, when a
critic rewards agreement over truth (see [failure modes](/concepts/failure-modes)). The demo's
final beat is exactly this: run the same loop with a critic that rewards a fawning, agreeable
answer, and it takes a *correct* reply ("the capital of Australia is Canberra") to a confidently
*wrong* one — the critic scores it **100%** while a held-out oracle the loop never sees scores it
**0%**. And **false confidence**: because the loop's success signal *is* the critic, a rising
score can look like progress while true quality is flat or falling. The bounds (iteration cap,
stall check) limit *effort*; they do nothing about a critic climbing the wrong hill.

## What complexity does it introduce?

A second role to design and maintain (the critic and its rubric), extra control flow that must
terminate (caps, stall detection, keep-the-best), and a new evaluation problem: if the critic is
a model, you now must validate *it*, the same way an LLM judge must be validated (see
[evaluation](/concepts/evaluation)). You also inherit a subtle bookkeeping rule — return the
best draft seen, never blindly the last, or a late regression ships. None of it is exotic, but
"we added reflection" quietly means "we own a critic, a rubric, a bounded loop, and a way to
check the loop actually helped."

## What simpler alternative exists?

In order of preference: a **better prompt** that gets the first draft right (often the "reflection"
was papering over a vague instruction); a **deterministic check** — a schema, a test, a
verifier — where correctness is mechanically checkable, which is both simpler and *independent* in
a way self-critique never is; or **external-tool feedback** instead of self-critique when you do
loop, so the grading signal comes from outside the model. Reach for a full self-critique loop only
when none of those express the task. A check you can trust beats a critic you have to second-guess.

## When should I use it?

When the first pass is reliably imperfect and judging is easier than generating: drafting-then-
polishing (writing, summaries), code that can be run against tests, structured output re-checked
against a schema, multi-constraint answers where a rubric catches misses. It's strongest when the
critic is **external** — tests, tools, a validated judge — because then the feedback is
independent. And use it when the added latency and cost are affordable and you can *measure*, on a
held-out set, that reflection raised true quality and not just the critic's opinion of it.

## When should I avoid it?

When a better prompt or a deterministic check already solves it; when the task is cheap to get
right in one pass; when you can't afford the extra latency/cost or the quality gain doesn't clear
it; and — most important — when the only available critic is the model grading itself on a task
where its blind spots and the critic's blind spots are the *same*. That's where reflection quietly
makes things worse: it will optimize its own score, sycophantically, while true quality drops. If
you can't measure the improvement independently, assume there wasn't one.

## Common misconceptions

- **"Reflection makes the agent a higher tier of autonomy."** No — it's a technique used *inside*
  a tool-using or stateful agent, like [planning](/concepts/planning). It changes *how* an output
  is produced, not *who* decides the next step.
- **"Self-critique verifies the output."** It does not. Reflection is the model improving its own
  work; **verification** is a *separate*, independent check that the work is correct. A model
  grading itself is not independent — that's the whole reason external verifiers and human review
  still matter.
- **"More iterations, better answer."** Returns diminish fast, and every extra pass is a chance for
  the critic to talk the producer *out* of a good answer. Bound the loop and keep the best draft.
- **"A higher critic score means higher quality."** Only if the critic is a faithful measure of the
  goal. Optimize a proxy hard enough and you get Goodhart's law — a better number, not a better
  answer (see [evaluation](/concepts/evaluation)).

## Autonomy, oversight, and risk

Reflection is often *sold* as a safety feature — "the system checks its own work" — and that
framing is the risk. Self-critique is a quality technique, **not independent assurance**: a model
grading its own output shares its blind spots, so a self-critique score is not admissible evidence
of correctness on its own, and a sycophantic critic can raise that score while true quality falls.
For higher-risk, regulated use cases the correctness signal must come from *outside* the loop — an
external verifier or tool where the answer is checkable, a held-out [evaluation](/concepts/evaluation)
the loop can't overfit, or a human reviewer at consequential decision points. This is where
reflection meets the [EU AI Act](/governance/eu-ai-act)'s human-oversight and accuracy expectations
for high-risk systems (and ISO 42001-style management processes): they are satisfied in practice by
evidence of an *independent* check and its records — never by "the model reviews itself."
Applicability depends on role, use case, and jurisdiction.

## Build it, then own it

The reflect-critique-revise harness is a few dozen readable lines:
[`reflect.ts`](https://github.com/ZeroCool989/agent-atlas/blob/main/src/lib/reflection/reflect.ts) —
a critic built from the eval harness applied inline, a bounded loop that keeps the best draft, and
a `bestOfN` for the multi-sample variant. The two fixtures in
[`demo.ts`](https://github.com/ZeroCool989/agent-atlas/blob/main/src/lib/reflection/demo.ts) are the
whole lesson in code: one where reflection drives a weak draft from 0/3 to a passing 3/3 (riding out
a mid-trace regression), and one where a sycophantic critic reaches 100% confidence on an answer a
held-out oracle scores 0%. Read them, then try the interview questions below.
